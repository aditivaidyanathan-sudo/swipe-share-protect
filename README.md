# Swipe, Share… Protect?
### An Analysis of Users' Preferences for Digital Safeguards and Consent Frameworks in Online Dating

**Author:** Aditi Vaidyanathan  
**Submitted:** August 2025

---

## Overview

This research investigates how digital consent mechanisms on dating platforms can be redesigned to better reflect the lived experiences of young adults engaged in intimate image exchange (IIE). Focusing on Bumble, Tinder, and Hinge, the study examines how users aged 18–29 perceive, negotiate, and respond to consent in emotionally and contextually sensitive situations.

The project challenges the dominant model of digital consent as a binary, one-time transaction, and proposes instead that consent in digital intimate contexts is **relational, evolving, and context-sensitive** — requiring infrastructure that adapts to shifting trust dynamics, emotional states, and relational phases.

---

## Research Questions

- **RQ1:** What behavioural patterns and decision-making processes influence young adults' consent practices in digital intimate exchanges?
- **RQ2:** How do users respond to different consent management mechanisms, and what features enhance their sense of control and privacy?
- **RQ3:** How can digital consent management tools be optimised to support user autonomy while preventing misuse of intimate images?

---

## Methodology

- **Approach:** Qualitative content analysis with inductive thematic coding
- **Sample:** 15 structured interviews with UK-based dating app users aged 18–29 (53.3% women, 46.6% men)
- **Platforms:** Bumble (100%), Hinge (60%), Tinder (46.7%)
- **Coding framework:** Four-category modal response schema — Supportive, Conditional, Sceptical, Uncertain/Neutral — applied across 12 interview questions per participant

---

## The CONTEXT Framework

The primary output of this research is the **CONTEXT framework** (Consent Tools for Exchange and Trust) — a modular, user-informed consent management architecture designed for dating platforms. It is structured around three interdependent pillars:

### Flexibility
Consent tools that adapt to relational context and evolving user needs:
- One-time view permissions
- Time-limited media access
- Tiered sharing permissions (e.g., match-only, trusted contact)
- Consent revocation and image recall

### Security
Features that protect users from harm before and after sharing:
- End-to-end encryption
- Anti-screenshot and download restriction technology
- Passive behavioural cues for detecting coercion
- Image deletion for all parties

### Legal and Ethical Compliance
Tools aligned with UK regulatory frameworks:
- GDPR-compliant audit trails and consent logs
- Context-aware consent prompts
- User-defined log visibility and data retention settings
- Alignment with the UK Online Safety Act 2023 and Criminal Justice and Courts Act 2015

CONTEXT is designed to be toggled and personalised by users, positioning consent not as a compliance checkbox but as an ongoing, supported, and visible practice embedded in the platform's interaction design.

---

## Key Findings

- **100%** of participants expressed baseline scepticism toward dating platforms, with many migrating to Signal, WhatsApp, or Telegram for intimate exchanges
- **93.3%** supported consent revocation tools (e.g., delete for everyone, time-limited access)
- **86.6%** endorsed consent-gated media access (e.g., blurred previews, approve-to-view)
- **53.3%** supported private consent logs, with caveats around surveillance and over-formalisation
- Participants consistently preferred **ambient, opt-in, just-in-time** consent tools over rigid or interruptive mechanisms
- AI-assisted moderation was viewed ambivalently — welcomed for risk detection, but with strong demand for human override and algorithmic transparency

## Relevance and Applications

This research sits at the intersection of **usable security**, **human-computer interaction (HCI)**, **platform governance**, and **digital rights**. The CONTEXT framework has practical implications for:

- Dating platform developers embedding privacy-by-design principles
- Policymakers seeking to operationalise the UK Online Safety Act 2023
- Researchers working on digital consent, IBSA prevention, and affect-aware security design
